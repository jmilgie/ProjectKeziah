No. 2: Reincarnation by Fred Keziah

The Long-Lost Masterpiece

Originally produced in May 1957, "Visual Metaphysics No. 2: Reincarnation" was created by Fred Keziah (1920-2010), a Navy-trained illustrator and animator who specialized in educational graphics and informational art. This chart represented a groundbreaking exploration of spiritual rebirth through avant-garde visual language.

The chart combines symbolic imagery of cycles, transformation, and eternal return—rendered in the distinctive palette and geometric precision characteristic of mid-century modern design. Each element serves as a visual meditation on the concept of souls passing from one life to the next.

This rare piece was self-published in Arlington, Virginia, where Keziah lived after his Navy service. It disappeared from public view for decades until recent archival research uncovered references to its existence, inspiring our ongoing digital recreation project.

The Concept of Visual Metaphysics

Fred S. Keziah referred to these posters as "information sheets," and they were ambitiously intended to encapsulate complex phenomena in a single visual chart. In the post-war era of the 1950s, Keziah's Visual Metaphysics series sought to visualize abstract philosophical concepts through the language of modern design. This second installment in the series explored reincarnation—the cyclical journey of the soul through multiple lifetimes.

The chart utilizes geometric shapes, symbolic color theory, and spatial relationships to communicate the eternal cycle of death and rebirth. The composition draws inspiration from Eastern philosophical traditions while employing the clean, modernist aesthetic that defined the 1950s design sensibility.

It's worth noting the eclectic subject matter of the series: the first chart covered UFOs and flying saucer lore, while the second tackled spiritual metaphysics. This reflects the milieu of the 1950s, when public fascination with both space-age mysteries (like UFOs) and esoteric spirituality (like reincarnation and karma) was on the rise. The Reincarnation chart would have been among relatively early visualizations of reincarnation concepts in the West, predating the New Age movement by a couple of decades.
