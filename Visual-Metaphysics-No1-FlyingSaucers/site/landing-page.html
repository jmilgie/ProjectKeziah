The Flying Saucers Chart

Originally produced in July 1956, "Visual Metaphysics No. 1: Flying Saucers" was created by Fred Keziah (1920-2010), a Navy-trained illustrator and graphics specialist who specialized in technical information visualization. This groundbreaking chart compiles 228 illustrated UFO shapes and related data in a single comprehensive reference.

The chart is densely packed with drawings depicting different flying saucer shapes, observed formations and flight maneuvers, as well as caricatures of alleged "space people" (alien beings). Alongside the illustrations are numerous tables of reported UFO characteristics—including listings of observed colors, sounds, speeds, and sizes of the objects.

This rare piece was self-published in Arlington, Virginia in 1957 (with a revised edition in 1958), and sold by mail order for $1.00 per copy. Keziah credited The Little Listening Post – a Washington, D.C. UFO newsletter – for assisting with "library research" in gathering the chart's content, situating this work in the active UFO research community of 1950s America.

The Concept of Visual Metaphysics

Fred S. Keziah referred to these posters as "information sheets," and they were ambitiously intended to encapsulate complex phenomena in a single visual chart. In the post-war era of the 1950s, Keziah's Visual Metaphysics series sought to visualize both mysterious and metaphysical phenomena through the language of modern design.

The Flying Saucers chart is essentially an early form of data visualization applied to UFO reports. It represents a methodical, almost scientific approach to the subject, with Keziah treating it much like a military recognition chart. The compilation draws on case reports from UFO literature and newsletters, distilling them into a systematic graphical classification system.

It's worth noting the eclectic subject matter of the series: the first chart covered UFOs and flying saucer lore, while the second (published in May 1957) tackled reincarnation and spiritual metaphysics. This reflects the milieu of the 1950s, when public fascination with both space-age mysteries and esoteric spirituality was on the rise. Keziah's unique contribution was applying a data-driven approach to these usually sensationalized topics.
